# Analysis: Financial Analyst
**Startup:** Paleo
**Date:** April 2026
**Feedback profile:** Brutal VC

---

## Diagnosis

Paleo's unit economics are the best thing they have on the table right now. An LTV:CAC ratio of 4.5x with a payback period of 5.4 months at this stage is solid — above the minimum 3x threshold that most Pre-Seed investors look for in B2B SaaS — and the 0% churn over 3 months at least provisionally validates the projected LTV. The critical problem is not in the unit economics: it's in the runway. With €15K monthly burn and 4 months of cash remaining, Paleo is operating in an existential risk zone. A round that takes two months longer than expected — which is common in early-stage investment — can be fatal. Post-round projections improve the scenario to 20 months of runway, which is adequate, but that assumes the round closes on time and under the expected conditions.

## Main problems

1. **Current runway is a negotiation threat** — 4 months of runway is not just an operational risk: it's a signal of weakness to the investor. When a founder negotiates from desperation, they accept worse terms. An experienced investor knows this and can use that data to pressure on valuation or terms. The team should have started the round with at least 6-8 months of runway.

2. **The LTV of €3,576 is based on 3 months of zero churn, not on real retention data** — LTV is calculated assuming a projected monthly churn rate. With only 3 months of history and 12 customers, that projection is extrapolation, not evidence. If churn rises to 3% monthly when you scale to 50-100 customers (which is common when exiting the pilot phase), the real LTV falls to €2,450 and the LTV:CAC ratio drops to 3.1x. Still acceptable, but the margin of error is small.

3. **The €800 CAC is calculated on manual outbound with founder effort** — The current acquisition cost includes the CEO's time doing direct sales, which is probably valued at €0 in the model. When you hire a sales rep at €40K-€50K/year, the real CAC per customer will rise. With a demo-to-customer sales cycle and a rep closing 2-3 customers per month, the CAC rises to €1,200-€1,500. That compresses the ratio to 2.4x-3x. Still viable, but the projections need to reflect this.

4. **No revenue expansion model (NRR)** — The financial model is built on flat recurring revenue of €149/month per customer. There's no upsell hypothesis: premium plans, additional modules, volume-based commissions. In B2B SaaS for restaurants, NRR (Net Revenue Retention) above 110% is achievable with add-ons. Without that, the model is defensive and limits the return potential for the investor.

5. **Growth projections aren't justified by the channel** — If the model projects 80 customers in 12 months from the current 12 (×6.7), that implies closing approximately 6 new customers per month consistently. With manual outbound as the only channel and a single founder selling, that requires either hiring salespeople quickly or scaling channels. Neither is detailed in the financial model with corresponding costs.

## Recommendations

1. **Accelerate round close or temporarily reduce burn** — If the close stretches beyond 6-8 weeks from now, you need to manage burn. Review whether there are expenses you can defer (tools, external services) to get to 6 months of runway before closing. That gives you negotiating power.

2. **Build LTV scenarios with different churn levels** — Present three scenarios: optimistic (1% monthly churn, LTV €4,900), base (2% churn, LTV €3,576), and conservative (3.5% churn, LTV €2,200). That demonstrates rigor and shows the model is robust even in the conservative scenario.

3. **Recalculate CAC with the real cost of the sales team** — In the 12-month projected model, include the salary of at least one sales rep and recalculate the resulting blended CAC. That makes the model more honest and prepares the investor for the reality of scaling sales.

4. **Add a revenue expansion layer to the model** — Define at least one upsell module (for example, advanced business analytics at an additional €49/month, or delivery platform integration at €29/month). Model that 30% of customers adopt it within the first 6 months. That pushes NRR above 105% and changes the retention narrative to expansion.

## Readiness score
**7/10**

Unit economics are the strongest point in Paleo's investor case right now. An LTV:CAC of 4.5x and payback of 5.4 months with zero churn over 3 months is a genuinely positive signal at this stage. What lowers the score is the critical runway that pressures the negotiation, LTV projections built on insufficient history, and a financial model that doesn't capture the impact of scaling the sales team or the revenue expansion potential. With a revised model incorporating these elements, the financial analysis goes from being a partial strength to being the most solid argument in the pitch.
