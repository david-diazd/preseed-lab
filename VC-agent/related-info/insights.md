# Key Insights for Pre-Seed Fundraising

Synthesis of the most important resources. Organized by topic to inform the construction of agents.

---

## 1. What investors evaluate at Pre-Seed

At pre-seed there is rarely enough data to evaluate metrics. Investors are betting on the founder, not the business.

The four key factors (in order of weight):

1. **Team** — do they have an unfair advantage? (domain expertise, prior exits, deep sector relationships)
2. **Problem** — is it real, painful, and large enough?
3. **Timing** — why now? What has changed in the world that makes this solution possible today?
4. **Traction signal** — one paying customer, strong retention, or a notable pilot matters enormously, even if small

> *"At the pre-seed stage, investors fund the founder, not the business."* — First Round Capital

---

## 2. Pitch deck: structure and criteria

- Optimal range: **10-15 slides**
- Investors spend **2-5 minutes** deciding whether they want a meeting
- Essential slides: problem, solution, market, traction, team, minimal financials, and current round

**What makes a good deck:**
- Non-obvious knowledge about the problem or customer that others don't yet have
- Bottom-up market calculation (show the reasoning, not just the number)
- Clear use of capital: "with this money we'll reach X milestone by Y date"
- If traction is weak → omit it; never include unimpressive metrics

**Narrative criterion:**
The deck must answer in order: *what is the pain? why this solution? why this team? why now?*

---

## 3. Market size

- Investors want to see **how you calculate TAM**, not the final number
- "Our TAM is $50B" without methodology adds nothing
- Bottom-up always beats top-down: start from the unit customer, not the global sector
- For Pre-Seed: a potential market of at least $1B is necessary to justify VC returns

---

## 4. Traction at Pre-Seed stage

Traction is not just revenue. It's demonstrating speed and momentum from the start.

**Valid signals at pre-seed:**
- Paying customers (even if few)
- Waitlist with conversions
- High retention (NRR, low churn)
- Letters of intent (LOIs) from customers or partners
- Consistent weekly growth (7-10% per week at early stage is exceptional)
- Active product usage

**Golden rule:** Show progress with time frames. Not just "we have 50 users" but "we went from 0 to 50 users in 6 weeks, with 80% retention."

---

## 5. Key unit economics

Financial agents should evaluate:

- **CAC** (Customer Acquisition Cost) — how much it costs to acquire a customer
- **LTV** (Lifetime Value) — total value of the customer
- **LTV:CAC ratio** — healthy >3x for SaaS
- **Payback period** — time to recover CAC (ideally <12 months at early stage)
- **Churn** — monthly and annual; <2% monthly is good in SaaS
- **MRR/ARR and growth** — the % of month-over-month growth matters, not the absolute
- **Burn rate** — net monthly spend
- **Runway** — months of life with current capital

## 6. The fundraising process (Paul Graham)

- **Raising money consumes all mental attention.** Enter "fundraising mode" and finish it quickly.
- **Contact investors in parallel**, weighted by probability of close × check size.
- **Investors use ambiguous language.** Only a concrete written offer counts.
- Valuation is the third priority: first sufficient capital, second investor quality.
- **Accept good offers when they appear** rather than waiting for better ones.
- Reaching "ramen profitability" before raising inverts the power dynamic.
- Don't raise more than you need: excess creates spending pressure and unsustainable expectations.

**Realistic timeline:** Founders close seed rounds in 11-15 weeks. Compress all meetings into 2-3 weeks to create urgency.

## 7. Most common founder mistakes

- Raising before you're ready (damages reputation with investors)
- Including poor metrics in the deck instead of omitting them
- Financial projections disconnected from reality or without basis
- Not knowing how to explain the TAM calculation
- Generic pitch without a "non-obvious insight" about the market
- Aggressively hiring right after closing the round
- Spending heavily before proving profitability
- Confusing investor validation with real traction

## 8. What a VC partner looks for to say yes

Decision process simulation (based on Secrets of Sand Hill Road and a16z):

1. Is the market large enough if the thesis plays out?
2. Does this team have an unfair advantage to win this market?
3. Why now? What has changed (technology, regulation, behavior) that makes it possible?
4. How defensible is the position long-term? (moats, network effects, data, switching costs)
5. What is the path to a >10x exit? (for the fund to have a return)

> VCs need fund returns: a €50M fund needs at least a €500M exit to return the fund 1x. Every investment must have that theoretical potential.

## 9. Pre-Seed valuation criteria

Factors that raise valuation:
- Founders with prior exits
- Proprietary technology or IP
- Strong early traction
- Very large and growing market
- Strategic competition between investors

Factors that lower it:
- Only a technical founder (no go-to-market)
- Small or saturated market
- No traction or validation signal
- Founders with no relevant domain experience

**Typical Pre-Seed ranges in Europe (2024-2025):**
- Pre-money valuation: €1M - €5M
- Round size: €150K - €750K
- Typical dilution: 10-20%

---

## 10. Pre-Seed readiness checklist

A founder is ready to raise when they can clearly answer:

- [ ] What is the problem and who suffers it specifically?
- [ ] Why is your solution better than existing alternatives?
- [ ] What is the total market and how do you calculate it bottom-up?
- [ ] What traction do you have and how long did it take to get it?
- [ ] Why this team? What is your unfair advantage?
- [ ] Why now? What has changed that makes this possible today?
- [ ] How much are you raising, at what valuation, and to reach what milestone?
- [ ] How many months of runway does that round give you?
- [ ] What are your current or projected unit economics?
- [ ] What is the go-to-market plan for the next 12 months?
